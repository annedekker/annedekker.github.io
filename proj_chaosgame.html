The Chaos Game

The chaos game is an algorithm described by the British mathematician Michael Barnsley in 1988. The rules are as follows:

  • Decide on a polgyon.
  • Place a point at random inside the polgyon.
  • For every turn, choose one of the corners of the polygon at random, and move the point a percentage of the distance to the corner.
  • Repeat the previous step indefinitely.

Optionally, you can also restrict the corner selection process, by excluding corners that are a certain amount of positions away from the corner(s) the point last moved towards.

This game is fun to play because it results in pretty patterns, and some of those patterns are fractals.

Fractals are never-ending patterns, that display the same type of structure on all levels (something that kind of looks like a fern on a big scale will be made of tiny ferns when you zoom in). Fractals are interesting because they contain indefinite complexity based on a simple instruction, in a way somewhat similar to early, primitive forms of life. Indeed, there are many fractals that look like plants. They are used to stimulate situations that aren't suited to exact measurement, like many in nature are. (Think, for example, about a coastline. If you're making a map, and you want to be as precise as possible, you'll have to map every centimeter by hand - and then start over the next day because it changed.)

When you play the game with the most famous settings (3 corners in a circle, move 50% of the distance) you create a fractal known as the Sierpinski triangle, the image you see at the top of this page.
